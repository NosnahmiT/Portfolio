Nonprofit Website Redesign
UX/UI Design Case Study

Jump To:

Phase 1: Research & Analysis Phase 2: Planning & Strategy Phase 3: Prototyping & Testing

Overview

In this project I was tasked with selecting a nonprofit with an outdated website and redesigning it to better suit responsive web design standards. I chose the Telluride Historical Museum. I undertook a comprehensive redesign of their website, modernizing the aesthetic, improving its navigation and functionality, while making it more accessible and responsive.

Tools Used

  • Figma
  • Invision
  • Miro
  • Google Workspace

Problem

While exploring nonprofits in Colorado (where I lived at the time), I came across the Telluride Historical Museum. Their website was out of date aesthically and not very functional across different devices. The lack of responsive design made it difficult to navigate and access important information across desktop, mobile, and tablet devices.

Role

  • UX Researcher
  • UX Designer
  • UI Designer

Solution

A responsive website redesign that aims to provide a more engaging and streamlined experience for visitors, with the goal of seamlessly delivering information and encouraging them to transition from online engagement to in-person museum visits.

Timeline

Three weeks.

Phase 1: Empathize & Research
Heuristic Evaluation

To kick things off, I conducted a heuristic evaluation of the museum's existing website. While it had all of the necessary information, its user experience fell short. Issues included inconsistent formatting, inadequate use of white space, and an overall unfriendly layout that failed to create an inviting online space. The website, in its current state, did not inspire a desire to spend extended periods exploring it, deterring potential museum visits. Determined to transform website visitors into museum attendees, my redesign focused on improving user engagement. The screenshots above depict the homepage and top navigation, revealing formatting issues such as an oversized logo and irregularities in the top navigation bar. Delving deeper, I identified problems such as text overlap and outdated content, reinforcing the need for both structural and aesthetic enhancements.

Stakeholder Contact

Following a thorough examination of the website's design and navigation, I reached out to museum employees via email, receiving a response from Molly Daniel, the Director of Exhibits and Programs. Her feedback provided useful insight, shaping the direction of the project. Some of her comments are shared below:

Outdated

"The website, in my opinion, serves its purpose but could definitely use an update."

Formatting

"The present formatting is limiting as far as interesting design layouts and interfacing."

Limited

"It being a WordPress site, and an old one at that, it requires a lot of plugins for additional features such as commerce."

Boring

"The design is straightforward, but from my perspective, pretty plain."

User Interviews

After reviewing the information I received from my museum stakeholder, I wanted to interview some museum-goers to better understand what they would be looking for in a museum's website. I found five people that enjoyed going to museums, set up some interviews, and asked them some questions. In the interviews, my aim was to uncover participants' preferences regarding museums, their visitation habits, expectations from a museum's website, and their perceptions of poorly formatted or outdated websites. The most notable insights from the interviews are shared below:

Need-to-know Info

100% expect to see hours, exhibits, prices, etc. clearly listed on the homepage.

Bad Sites

100% aren't eager to visit a place if it has a poorly designed website.

Formatted Frustration

100% quickly lose patience for poorly formatted, hard to use websites.

Affinity Diagram & Empathy Map

After reaching out to stakeholders and conducting user interviews, I aggregated the data and organized it into an affinity diagram. This step was crucial in identifying connections and commonalities among the interviewed users. Uncovering these patterns facilitated the development of a comprehensive User Persona, providing insights into users' preferences and interactions with museum websites. To enhance this process, I further refined the understanding by creating an empathy map, simplifying the extraction of persona traits from the interview data.

Phase 2: Definition & Ideation
Storyboard

With a solidified user persona, I proceeded to develop a storyboard. The following narrative illustrates Janet's challenges with the existing website and demonstrates how my redesign effectively addresses and solves those issues.

User Insight

Having defined a concept and created a persona, I began to develop a user insight and problem statement. The user insight featured user perspectives but also business insight. Recognizing that the museum sells an experience, the emphasis was on the website serving as a catalyst to drive online visitors toward physically visiting the building.

"Janet Snakehole's need to quickly access and view museum information is critical as it increases the likelihood of her visiting the museum while she is in Telluride. Websites that are outdated and poorly formatted cause frustration, prompting users to leave sooner than desired, diminishing their likelihood of visiting the museum in person. Therefore, the website's design directly influences the user's decision to engage with the museum physically."

How Might We?

How might we update the website's design, improve its RWD, and enhance functionality to create a more digestible and streamlined way to deliver information, with the aim of attracting more visitors to the museum?

Problem Statement

The Telluride Historical Museum website is designed to communicate essential information and services to potential visitors. However, it is evident that the current design lacks proper responsiveness, impeding navigation and information accessibility across desktop, mobile, and tablet devices. Our objective is to revamp the website's design, focusing on improving its responsiveness across different devices. By doing so, we aim to create a more seamless and user-friendly experience, ensuring that potential visitors can easily access information about the museum.

Value Proposition

Vistor Centric

FEATURES

  • Easy Ticket Reservation
  • Faster path to contact page
  • Easily research current exhibits
  • Updated aesthetic

BENEFITS

  • Potential visitors will spend less time struggling on the site and more time looking at what the museum has to offer.

Next, I crafted a value proposition for the redesign: "I am undertaking the redesign of the Telluride Historical Museum's website to facilitate a straightforward and efficient process for potential visitors to locate and access relevant information, aiding them in deciding whether to visit the museum or not." This, combined with the value proposition canvas, shows how the redesign aims to enhance the visitor experience on the website.

Phase 3: Prototyping & Testing
Sitemap

With a clear vision of the website's user-centric goals, I initiated the process of structuring the redesign. Beginning with card sorting, I deconstructed the existing navigation structure and reconstructed it to enhance user-friendliness. This involved eliminating duplicate pages, inactive links, and obsolete event pages from years past. The resulting site structure streamlined pathways and removed unnecessary pages, enabling users to efficiently research the museum, its attractions, prices, guidelines, and more. The revamped structure, coupled with a new aesthetic, aimed to present the museum in a more serious and professional light.

Task & User Flows

With a new website structure established, I built out task and user flows to outline potential interactions with the site. This was an important step to take before beginning on wireframes. With these and the sitemap I knew what all needed to go into the website before I began building out any of the screens. In the gallery above, three user flows and three concise task flows are presented, providing a visual representation of the anticipated user journey and key task interactions.

Mid-Fidelity Prototype

Drawing early inspiration from prominent museum websites such as MoMA, Denver Art Museum, and the Portland Art Museum, I aimed to infuse the redesign with a captivating, informative, and serious aesthetic. The complete inspiration board can be viewed here. As I delved into developing the mid-fidelity prototype, I focused on capturing the user's interest from the moment they entered the site. The initial visual encounter would feature a prominent image of the San Juan Mountain Range, the home of Telluride, accompanied by the phrase, "Discover the Geological Wonder of the San Juans." This approach aimed to engage users and encourage them to explore further, fostering a curiosity about what the museum has to offer. The desktop prototype is accessible here, and the mobile version can be explored here.

Style Guide & Inspiration

The color palette for this redesign was inspired by a fascinating exhibit at the museum; 'The Telluride Blanket.' Carbon-dated to have been crafted between 1041-1272 C.E. by the Ancestral Puebloan peoples, the blanket showcased beautiful colors and a rich history. Recognizing its significance, I integrated its colors into the redesign, becoming the driving force behind the site's new aesthetic. The new logo incorporated these colors and patterns, with a clean and crisp text using the Sans-Serif font, Roboto. This thoughtful color palette not only contributed to a cohesive style guide but also imbued the website with an elevated and modern appearance. The gallery showcases the finalized style guide.

High Fidelity Prototype

After completing the mid-fidelity prototypes and finalizing the style guide, I conducted usability tests to evaluate the effectiveness of the site. These tests provided insights into what was working and what needed improvement. Based on the findings, I made updates to the sites navigation and aesthetic. Following the completion of testing and the style guide, I proceeded to develop high-fidelity desktop and mobile prototypes, bringing the project to life by integrating colors and styles into the mid-fidelity structures.

The finished product represents a significant departure from the museum's original website. This updated redesign aims to better encapsulate the museum's offerings in a clean, simple, and modern manner. Stripped of unnecessary embellishments, it provides visitors with essential information through straightforward navigation and an updated, clean aesthetic.
